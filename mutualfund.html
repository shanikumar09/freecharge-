What is a Mutual Fund?

Funds collected from various stakeholders including individual investors and companies is termed as a Mutual Fund. This fund is handled by a Fund Manager who invests the money through different avenues to get the best returns for the investments. The money could be invested in stocks, bonds, shares, securities and other financial products that provide the best opportunities for financial growth. Each Fund Manager will have a certain goal which will be dependent upon the types of Mutual Funds he/she manages.

Most Fund Managers are part of Fund Houses like Axis Bank Mutual Fund or Motilal Oswal Mutual Fund. These companies are responsible for investing the shared pool of money in the Mutual Funds to buy financial instruments.

Benefits of investing in
Mutual Funds

Higher returns

Compared to other common investment options, Mutual Fund investments provide better returns over similar periods.

Low Cost

With Mutual Funds, investors can access large portfolios at lower investment values. In certain Mutual Funds, investors can invest periodic sums that are as low as Rs.100.

SIP option

Mutual Fund investments offer investors the flexibility of investing smaller amounts - SIP(Systematic Investment plan) periodically, as opposed to lump-sum payments that might not be feasible for all investors.

Goal-based investment

Another advantage of Mutual Funds is that investors can invest in different types of Mutual Funds according to their personal goals. From buying a house or a car to securing children’s education, people can select a Mutual Fund according to their financial needs.

Diversification

Since the money from a Mutual Fund is invested in different assets and shares, the risk of losing money on Mutual Funds is really low. Even if an asset shows losses, gains from other assets will reduce the losses

Tax-benefit

Certain types of Mutual Funds like ELSS (Equity Linked Saving Schemes) are tax-saving Mutual Funds that can help with tax deductions under Section 80C of the Income Tax Act. Thus, these Mutual Funds have double the benefit, tax deductions and wealth creation.

Security

Mutual Funds in India are regulated by SEBI (Securities Exchange and Board of India) and falls under the purview of the Association of Mutual Funds in India (AMFI). Since the regulatory body is a government agency, investor interests in Mutual Funds will be safeguarded.

Types of Mutual Funds

Understanding the different types of Mutual Funds is critical to
selecting the right type of Mutual Fund for your needs.

The broad category of Mutual Funds are

1.Equity Funds - Higher risk, higher returns

As the name suggests, Equity Funds are Mutual Funds in which the funds collected are invested in the shares of many companies. Equity Funds are highly attuned to market conditions. If the shares of a company reduce, the fund suffers a loss and when the shares of a company rise, the Equity Fund makes a profit. Fund Managers invest in equities from different companies to reduce the risk. But even then, Equity Funds are moderate to higher risk Mutual Funds compared to Debt and Hybrid Mutual Funds.

An example of an Equity Fund is Kotak Select Focus Fund.

The Kotak Standard Multi Cap Fund is a Mutual Fund that mostly has investments in equities and related securities. The main goal of Kotak Select Focus Fund is to increase capital in the long-term.

Equity Funds can be further classified according to the percentage of the total asset in the Mutual Fund invested in different company equities according to the company size.

The types of Equity Funds are

Large Cap

To be termed a Large Cap fund, a minimum of 80% of the total money in the Mutual Fund should be invested in companies of large size.

Example of a Large Cap fund - Axis Bluechip Fund

Multi Cap

To be termed a Multi Cap fund, a minimum of 65% of the total money in the Mutual Fund should be invested in large, small and medium-sized companies.

Example of a Multi Cap fund - Axis Focused 25 Fund

Mid Cap

To be termed a Mid Cap fund, a minimum of 65% of the total money in the Mutual Fund should be invested in companies of medium size or capital.

Example of a Mid Cap fund - Axis Mid Cap Fund

Small Cap

Small Cap funds are those funds in which at least 65% of the total assets are invested in small-sized companies.

Example of a Small Cap fund - Axis Small Cap Fund

2.Debt Funds - Low risk, low returns

Compared to Equity Funds, Debt Funds are far less risky since the main investments in this type of Mutual Fund are Treasury Bills, Treasury Notes and other such government securities. Other investment avenues include money market instruments, corporate bonds and commercial papers. Even the goal of Debt Funds varies from Equity Funds, looking to give stable, risk-free returns as opposed to long-term capital appreciation. But the disadvantage of these funds is that their rate of return is quite low. But for short term goals like buying a car or planning a vacation, Debt Fund Mutual Funds are ideal.

An example of a Debt Fund is Aditya Birla Sun Life Government Securities Fund from Aditya Birla Sun Life Mutual Fund. This fund is invested exclusively in government securities.

Debt Funds also can be categorised further into -

Liquid Funds

One of the favourite Mutual Fund investments for first-time investors, Liquid Funds are Debt Funds with very low risk. This Mutual Fund invests money in short-term market instruments such as government securities and treasury with a maturity of upto 91 days. The biggest advantage of this type of Mutual Fund is that it provides easy liquidity. Also in the current situation, it provides higher returns than a Savings Account or Fixed Deposit.

Example of Liquid Fund - Axis Liquid Fund

Money Market Funds

These Mutual Funds are similar to Liquid Funds except for their maturity period which tends to be a maximum of 1 year. Much like Liquid Funds, Money Market Funds are relatively less risky.

Corporate Bond Funds

These Mutual Funds invest a minimum of 80% of the total fund amount in corporate bonds which are highly rated.

Gilt Funds

These funds invest in highly-rated, low credit risk, government securities (both Central and State). Most investors who are averse to taking risks, choose Gilt Funds since government securities have a very low default rate

Duration-based Funds

These are Debt Funds with varying periods of maturity. From Long Duration Funds that average at about 10 years of maturity to Ultra Short Duration Funds that mature in 1 to 2 months. Other Duration-based funds include Medium and Short Duration Funds.

3.Hybrid Funds - Medium risk, medium returns

Also known as Balanced Funds, Hybrid Funds are a mix of equities and debt instruments. This combination helps balance out the risk posed by certain investments and helps control the rate of return. The shares to bonds ratio in a Hybrid Fund is determined by the Fund Manager.

An example of a Hybrid Fund is SBI Equity Hybrid Fund. It is an Aggressive Hybrid Fund with about 72% of the investment in Indian stocks and about 21% invested in debt instruments like Government securities.

The classification of Hybrid Funds include -

Aggressive

In this category of Hybrid Funds, 65-85% of the money is invested in equities while 20-35% is invested in debt instruments.

Conservative

In this category of Hybrid Funds, 10-25% of the money is invested in equities while 75-90% is invested in debt instruments.

Balanced

In this category of Hybrid Funds, the allocation of funds to both equities and debt instruments are almost the same.

Dynamic Asset Allocation

As the name suggests, the assets of the Mutual Fund are allocated dynamically across equities and debt instruments by the Fund Manager to get the best returns.

How to save taxes with Mutual Funds - ELSS

Another favourite Mutual Fund investment option among investors who have just started investing in Mutual Funds are tax-saving Mutual Funds. ELSS ( Equity Linked Savings Schemes) is the best Mutual Fund to invest in for anyone thinking of reducing their tax burden.

The biggest draw of ELSS is that it not just helps save on tax but also creates wealth at the same time for the investor. ELSS is an Equity Fund, which means many of the assets allocated in the Mutual Fund are invested in the equities market.

Benefits of investing in tax-saving Mutual Funds

Tax benefit under Section 80 C

With ELSS you are qualified to claim up to Rs.1,50,000 deduction from your gross taxable income. Unlike other savings schemes like FD, PPF and NSC, investors who choose the dividend option for their ELSS can get their dividends without paying taxes. Also, when they sell their units, they don’t have to pay long term capital gains tax.

Higher interest rates than FD/PPF

Compared to saving schemes like FD/PPF, ELSS gives higher returns.

Lowest lock-in period

Among all the tax-saving instruments, ELSS funds have the lowest lock-in period of 3 years.

Monthly investment option

Unlike an FD, with ELSS funds you can opt for Systematic Installment Plan (SIP) with which you can make your Mutual Fund investment at a leisurely pace without having to struggle for the necessary funds.

No maturity deadline

You can liquidate your ELSS fund after the mandatory lock-in period without. You don’t have to wait for the Mutual Fund to reach maturity.

A Guide To Systematic
Investment Plans (SIP)

Since it is difficult for individual investors to collect the required amount of money for investing in Mutual Funds in one go, they prefer to invest in their Mutual Funds through SIP. SIP allows investors to put in money regularly like weekly, monthly or quarterly.

Here are the benefits of investing through SIP -

  • Encourages disciplined investing that’s not dependent on market conditions
  • Investing through SIP helps in earning returns on the principal amount and on the gains made on the principal amount
  • With SIP, the average cost of holding is lower.
  • The amount to be invested can be planned instead of investing one time through lump sum investment.
